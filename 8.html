Design meaninful signifiers

Date: 2/11/18

Author: Clément CARMINATI

Nowadays, signifiers are everywhere on phones, cars, or in a kitchen, you can find them everywhere, but are they really meaningful?
When you are designing a thing and you need to indicate the user of the control of a function, the first thing that comes into your mind is to add a signifier: a little icon or a text next to the thing you want to show the function. Donald Normans explain in "The Design of Everyday Things" how bad signifiers can be.

Indeed a signifier has to be interpreted by the user and this interpretation can change between different types of users, that is why UX design is so complex.

Also, signifiers will determine the affordance of your object. Affordances are an object’s properties that show the possible actions users can take with it, thereby suggesting how they may interact with that object. For instance, a button can look as if it needs to be turned or pushed. The characteristics of the button which make it look “turnable” or “pushable” together form its affordances. Like D.Norman said "Affordance has to exist, it is an implicit property of an object"

And here is where all designer should focus: Do an object need a signifier? or is it reliable enough to show his affordance through his shape? For example, a door without handle should be pushed. It doesn't need the text "push" in it, still how many users will be confused by that door?
